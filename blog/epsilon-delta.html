Some Example Epsilon-Delta Proofs

The Epsilon-Delta Definition of a Limit

For these examples, given some set \(A \subseteq \RR\) which acts as the domain of a function \(f : A \lra \RR\) we define:

\[ \lim_{x \ra a} f(x) = L\]

To mean:

For all \( \epsilon > 0 \), there exists some \( \delta > 0 \) such that for every \(x \in A \) if \( 0 < |x - a| < \delta \), then \( | f(x) - L | < \epsilon \).

Example 0

Show that:

\[ \lim_{x \ra a} f(x) = L \]

Proof:

  1. I fix some arbitrary \(\epsilon > 0\)
  2. I choose \(\delta = \:???\)
  3. I show that \(\delta > 0\)
  4. I fix some arbitraty \(x \in A\)
  5. I assume that \(0 < |x-a|<\delta\)
  6. I prove that \(|f(x)-L|<\epsilon\)
  7. I conclude that the proof is complete.

This is the general structure of an epsilon-delta proof. Each step corresponsed to a portion of the definition. If you can't see why each step of the general solution is nessecary, then you will have to review the basics of writing proofs, and especially how to use quantifiers.

It's worth mentioning that in the literature, typically Step 3 and 4 aren't explicitly stated because they are either obvious or not seen as nessecary. However, for a complete argument I believe that they are technically nessecary, and so I will include them in all of my own example proofs.

Example 1

Show that:

\[ \lim_{x \ra 1} x + 1 = 2 \]

Proof:

  1. I fix some arbitrary \(\epsilon > 0\)
  2. I choose \(\delta = \epsilon\)
  3. Because of (1) and (2), it's true that \(\delta > 0\)
  4. I fix some arbitraty \(x \in \RR\)
  5. I assume that \(0 < |x-1|<\delta\)
  6. Because of (2) and (5), it's true that \(|x-1|<\epsilon\)
  7. Thus \(|(x + 1) - 2| < \epsilon\)
  8. I conclude that the proof is complete.
Here, you'll notice I mentioned the lines used to justify certain steps right in the proof. This level of detail is usually not nessecary when writing a math proof. But, because this is meant to help you understand the proofs, I will avoid skipping steps as much as I can.

Example 2

Show that:

\[ \lim_{x \ra 0} x^2 = 0 \]

Proof:

  1. I fix some arbitrary \(\epsilon > 0\)
  2. I choose \(\delta = \sqrt{\epsilon}\)
  3. Because of (1) and (2), it's true that \(\delta > 0\)
  4. I fix some arbitraty \(x \in \RR\)
  5. I assume that \(0 < |x-0|<\delta\)
  6. Because of (2) and (5), it's true that \(|x-0| < \sqrt{\epsilon}\)
  7. Thus \( |x| < \sqrt{\epsilon}\)
  8. Thus \(|x|^2 < \left( \sqrt{\epsilon} \right)^2 \)
  9. Thus \( |x^2| < \epsilon \)
  10. Thus \( |x^2 - 0| < \epsilon \)
  11. I conclude that the proof is complete.

At this point, I want to talk about how we choose \(\delta\) in step 2 of the proof. Typically, we do this by doing the proof in reverse as rough. I will now show how that's done for this proof.

Rough Work:

  • \( |x^2 - 0| < \epsilon \)
  • \( |x^2| < \epsilon \)
  • \( |x|^2 < \epsilon \)
  • \( \sqrt{|x|^2} < \sqrt{\epsilon} \)
  • \( |x| < \sqrt{\epsilon} \)
  • \( |x - 0| < \sqrt{\epsilon} \)
From now on, I will put the rough work before the proof to show the full work that goes into epsilon-delta proofs.

Exercises

  1. Do the rough work for Example 1.

Example 3

Show that:

\[ \lim_{x \ra 3} x^2 - 9 = 0 \]

Rough Work:

  • \( |x^2 - 9| < \epsilon \)
  • \( |x + 3| |x - 3| < \epsilon \)
The strategy here is to split the inequality into two as follows:
  • \( |x + 3| |x - 3| < C \frac{\epsilon}{C} \)
  • \( |x - 3| < \frac{\epsilon}{C} \)
  • \( |x + 3| < C \)
Now we just have to figure out a suitable choice for \(C\). We do this by making sure that \(\delta \leq 1\). This allows us to do the following.
  • \( |x-3| < 1 \)
  • \( -1< x-3 < 1 \)
  • \( 2 < x < 4 \)
  • \( 5 < x + 3 < 7 \)
  • \( -7 < 5 < x + 3 < 7 \)
  • \( |x + 3| < 7 \)
From here it's clear that we should choose \(C = 7\). By reading the proof you can understand why what we did will end up working.

Proof:

  1. I fix some arbitrary \(\epsilon > 0\)
  2. I choose \(\delta = \min\{1, \frac{\epsilon}{7}\}\)
  3. Because of (1) and (2), it's true that \(\delta > 0\)
  4. I fix some arbitraty \(x \in \RR\)
  5. I assume that \(0 < |x-3|<\delta\)
  6. Because of (2), it's true that \(\delta \leq \frac{\epsilon}{7}\)
  7. Because of (5) and (6), it's true that \(|x-3|<\frac{\epsilon}{7}\)
  8. Because of (2), it's true that \(\delta \leq 1\)
  9. Because of (5) and (8), it's true that \(|x-3| < 1\)
  10. Thus \(-1 < x-3 < 1\)
  11. Thus \(-1 + 6 < x - 3 + 6 < 1 + 6\)
  12. Thus \( -7 < 5 < x + 3 < 7\)
  13. Thus \( |x + 3| < 7\)
  14. Because of (7) and (13), it's true that \(|x+3||x-3| < 7\frac{\epsilon}{7}\)
  15. Thus \(|x^2-9| < \epsilon\)
  16. I conclude that the proof is complete.

Exercises

  1. Prove that \( \lim_{x \ra 2} x^2 - 4 = 0 \)
  2. Find a different value of \(C\) you can use to prove Example 3.
  3. Prove that \( \lim_{x \ra 2} x^2 - 3 = 1 \)
