An Epsilon Delta Proof


In which William goes to great lengths to explain a single proof.

Introduction


In this short article, my goal is to present a fairly simple epsilon-delta proof and then break it down in as much detail as possible. My hope is to give readers an insight into exactly what goes into mathematical thinking. By having a deep understanding of each step, I hope the reader can construct their own epsilon-delta proofs with confidence that they are correct and make sense.


I have chosen to prove the following. (Assume every variable is a real number unless otherwise specified.)


Theorem

Given any functions \(f : \RR \lra \RR\) and \(g : \RR \lra \RR\). If \(\lim_{x \ra a} f(x) = L\) and \(\lim_{x \ra a} g(x) = M\), then \(\lim_{x \ra a} f(x) + g(x) = L + M\).


This is a pretty simple result, but because it's a general result about any two functions, it will make for a more interesting and difficult proof. To begin, I will present the proof in full.


Proof

Let \(f : \RR \lra \RR\) and \(g : \RR \lra \RR\). Now assume \(\lim_{x \ra a} f(x) = L\) and \(\lim_{x \ra a} g(x) = M\). By definition, this means that for every there exists a \(\delta_1 > 0 \) such that for each \(x \in \RR\) if \(0 < | x - a | < \delta_1 \), then \(|f(x) - L| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} \). Also, there exists a \(\delta_2 > 0 \) such that for each \(x \in \RR\) if \(0 < | x - a | < \delta_2 \), then \(|g(x) - M| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} \). I now choose \(\delta = \min(\delta_1, \delta_2)\) and assume \(0 < |x - a| < \delta\). This means \(|f(x) - L| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} \) and \(|g(x) - M| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} \). So it follows that \[ | (f(x) + g(x)) - (L + M) | \leq |f(x) - L| + |g(x) - M| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} + \frac{\epsilon}{2} = \epsilon \] as required.
